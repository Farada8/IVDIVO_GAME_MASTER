## 1. Main studio verdict

The current visual concepts are strong as illustrations, but still weak as playable boards.

Estimated score:

- Illustration / visual hook: 9–10/10.
- Game board as playable system: 4–5/10.

Reason: the concepts invite players to look at the board, but do not yet strongly invite players to play. The board looks like a game, but the engine is not visible enough: why the player moves, what they collect, what they risk, why one route is better than another, how portals work, how cards drive play, and where the excitement comes from.

## 2. Core design correction

Do not design the next board as a decorative route.

Design it as a small living world.

For v0.1, “living world” does not mean a giant sandbox or hundreds of rules. It means visible interactive objects:

- Sparks placed on the board.
- Crystals placed on the board.
- Keys and portal markers.
- Random chest tokens.
- Wrecked ships / ruins that can be searched.
- Portal nodes that can open, close, break, or change links.
- Hero/NPC spaces that offer help, quests, or trades.
- Mission slots that direct players to different zones.
- Catastrophe track that pressures the session.

## 3. Game Board Review Matrix

| Criterion | Question | Current risk | v0.1 target |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Gameplay Readability | Can the player understand the board in 30 seconds? | Too much decoration, not enough hierarchy. | Goal, routes, spaces, resources and dangers are immediately clear. |
| Decision Density | Does the player choose every 1–2 turns? | Roll-and-move loop. | Choose route, risk, card, portal, resource or mission. |
| Excitement | Does the board create “I want to go there”? | Rewards are not visible enough. | Sparks, chests, crystals, heroes and gates must be visible. |

| Reward | Does the player receive frequent joy? | Bonuses are abstract. | Physical tokens and cards create constant small rewards. |

| Portal Meaning | Are portals more than teleporters? | Beautiful but unclear. | Each portal has color, cost, risk, bonus, and break condition. |

| Family Flow | Is it fast and accessible? | Risk of too many icons. | 5-minute teach, 60–90 sec turns, no long punishment. |

## 4. Five mandatory layers

The game only works if all five layers are present:

1. Route with distinct space types.
2. Resources: Sparks, Crystals, Keys, Treasures.
3. Portals with cost, risk, bonus, and possible breakage.
4. Cards: Portals, Surprise/Artifacts, Heroes, Missions, Treasures, Catastrophes.
5. Characters with different abilities and small player boards.

If one layer is missing, the design falls back into a beautiful roll-and-move board.

## 5. What must be visible on the next board

The next board art brief must not ask for “a beautiful board” only. It must require functional readability.

Required visible components:

- Space types: Spark, Crystal, Chest, Portal, Surprise, Hero, Trap, Element, Wreck, Ruins, Treasure, Gate, Central Portal.
- Physical tokens: Sparks, colored Crystals, Keys, Chests, portal markers.
- Five deck zones: Portals, Surprise/Artifacts, Heroes, Missions, Treasures.
- Player boards: character portrait, health, cards, Sparks, Crystals, Keys, active helpers.
- Catastrophe / Storm track.
- Central Portal energy track.
- Routes with forks: short dangerous path vs long safe path.
- Each island/zone must have a unique gameplay function.
- Game objects must look playable, not only decorative.

## 6. Board objects that should become mini-game nodes

### Chests

Not only decoration. Use face-down chest tokens. Outcomes may include treasure, trap, crystal, artifact, portal fragment, or mini-event.

### Wrecked ships

A separate search/repair node. Player may find a captain, map, monster, treasure, trap, pirates, or mission clue.

### Ancient tree / shrine / tower / temple

These are not decorative locations. They should offer choices: pay Sparks, gain a Spirit card, climb for better reward, place a Crystal, or receive a blessing.

### Dangerous locations

Volcano, bridge, cave, ice fortress, storm route, or whirlpool should be risk/reward decisions, not automatic penalties.

## 7. Portal formula

Each portal must have:

- color;
- entry;
- exit;
- opening cost;
- risk;
- bonus;
- break or instability condition.

Example:

**Fire Portal**  
Cost: 2 Sparks or 1 red Crystal.  
Effect: move to a fire-linked portal.  
Risk: roll the portal/element die.  
Bonus: if you have the Fire-friendly character/helper, ignore the risk.  
Break: on failure, the portal closes for one round.

This creates the core question: go safely by long route or jump through a risky shortcut?

## 8. Cards and decks

For v0.1, keep the physical deck count low, but keep future expansion space.

### v0.1 decks

1. Portals.
2. Surprise / Artifacts / Treasures.
3. Heroes.
4. Missions.
5. Catastrophes.

### Future expansion reserve

- Monsters.
- Trials.
- Allies.
- Elemental Spirits.
- Legends.
- Secrets.
- Curses.
- Epic Bosses.
- Mini-quests.

These should not become separate piles in v0.1, but can be Kickstarter expansion and franchise material.

## 9. Final corrected direction

The project should not become a decorative roll-and-move game.

It should become a bright family adventure race where:

- players collect visible resources;
- every route has a reason;
- portals are risky shortcuts;
- cards constantly change the map;
- missions create direction;
- heroes create identity;
- catastrophe creates pressure;
- the board looks like a living world.

Final formula:

**Portals** is not a walk across a beautiful map. It is a small living world where players collect Sparks and Crystals, open risky portals, draw adventure cards, interact with heroes and board objects, complete short missions, and race or cooperate to activate the Central Portal before Catastrophe arrives.
